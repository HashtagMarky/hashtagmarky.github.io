**There's more than one version of Morgan le Fay that history has reduced to a villain, but this book is the first time it felt like Sophie Keetch has let her come across that way.** *Storm Over Camelot* is where the Morgan Le Fay Trilogy reaches its conclusion, in a complicated, satisfying, genuinely well earned way.

Morgan is not at her most likeable here in the first act. She's harsher, colder, and less willing to extend the grace she was never given to begin with. But after everything she's been through across this trilogy, I was never frustrated with her, I knew that if I were in her position, I would be no better.

Morgan is in a terrible situation, even living well protected in Bella Garde. She struggles in so many ways, but most dearly, with her own identity, something we hadn't seen so drastically up to this point. She wins, she loses, she learns, and that latter is when she is at her best. Everything Morgan finds out about the world she starts to again take in her stride, ramping up throughout the second act, giving her peace and closure, until she is the fully fledged heroine you cannot see as anything else.

And this finale does what any good ending should. The pay off to everything the series built is immaculate, without deviating from the tragedy that was always a part of this mythical tale. The version of Morgan we see by the end of this book is completely recognisable as the figure of legend, at her best and worst, but now you understand exactly how and why she got there.

Sophie Keetch, that was a stunning end to an amazing trilogy.
